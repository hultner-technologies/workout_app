# Evidence-Based Athletic Development Programs

This document provides comprehensive information on five respected strength and conditioning programs designed for athletic development, including olympic lifting and barbell training methodologies.

---

## 1. Westside for Skinny Bastards (WS4SB)

### Name
Westside for Skinny Bastards (WS4SB)

### Author/Source
**Joe DeFranco** - Strength coach and owner of DeFranco's Gym

**Official Sources:**
- [Westside for Skinny Bastards Part I](https://www.defrancostraining.com/westside-for-skinny-bastards-part1/)
- [Westside for Skinny Bastards Part II](https://www.defrancostraining.com/westside-for-skinny-bastards-part2/)
- [Westside for Skinny Bastards Part III](https://www.defrancostraining.com/westside-for-skinny-bastards-part3/)
- [Original T-Nation Article](https://archive.t-nation.com/training/westside-for-skinny-bastards-1/)

### Description

Westside for Skinny Bastards is a modified version of the famous Westside Barbell Conjugate Method, adapted specifically for athletes, hardgainers, and younger lifters who need to build both strength and muscle mass. Created by renowned strength coach Joe DeFranco in 2008, this program takes the principles used by elite powerlifters and modifies them for the general athletic population.

The program addresses a common problem: many young athletes and "skinny bastards" (hardgainers) struggle to build size and strength using traditional bodybuilding or powerlifting programs. WS4SB bridges this gap by combining max effort strength work, repetition work for hypertrophy, and strategic exercise selection that won't interfere with sport-specific training.

One of the key innovations of WS4SB is replacing the traditional dynamic effort upper body day with a repetition effort day. DeFranco found that younger athletes and hardgainers respond better to higher-rep work for building muscle mass in the upper body, while still utilizing max effort work to build absolute strength. The program also eliminates the need for specialized equipment like chains and bands that are staples of traditional Westside training, making it more accessible to the average gym-goer.

The program has evolved through three versions (Parts I, II, and III), with Part III introducing a more traditional 4-day Westside split that includes dynamic effort lower body work for more advanced trainees.

### Structure

**Original 3-Day Split:**
- Day 1: Max Effort Upper Body
- Day 2: Max Effort Lower Body
- Day 3: Repetition Effort Upper Body

**WS4SB Part III (4-Day Split):**
- Monday: Max Effort Upper Body
- Tuesday: Dynamic Effort Lower Body
- Wednesday: Off
- Thursday: Repetition Effort Upper Body
- Friday: Max Effort Lower Body

The program uses the conjugate method approach of rotating max effort exercises every 2-3 weeks to prevent neural burnout and continue strength gains.

### Session Breakdown

#### Day 1: Max Effort Upper Body

**A. Max Effort Lift** - Work up to a max set of 3-5 reps
- Exercise rotation (change every 2-3 weeks):
  - Thick or regular bar bench press
  - Floor press
  - Rack lockout
  - Board press
  - Incline bench press (various angles)
  - Close grip bench press
  - Decline bench press
- Sets: Work up from ~50% 1RM, adding weight each set
- Reps: 3-5 reps (until you can no longer complete 3 reps)
- Rest: 3-5 minutes

**B. Supplemental Lift**
- Examples: Dumbbell bench press, dumbbell incline press, military press
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 6-10
- Rest: 2-3 minutes

**C. Horizontal Rowing**
- Examples: Barbell rows, dumbbell rows, seated cable rows, chest-supported rows
- Sets: 4
- Reps: 10-15
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Rear Delt/Upper Back**
- Examples: Face pulls, rear delt flyes, band pull-aparts
- Sets: 2-3
- Reps: 12-15
- Rest: 60-90 seconds

**E. Weighted Abdominals**
- Examples: Weighted sit-ups, cable crunches, weighted side bends
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 8-15
- Rest: 60 seconds

#### Day 2: Max Effort Lower Body (or Day 5 in 4-day version)

**A. Max Effort Lift** - Work up to a max set of 5 reps
- Exercise rotation (change every 2-3 weeks):
  - Box squats (various bar types: regular, safety squat bar, cambered bar, buffalo bar)
  - Free squats (various bar types)
  - Straight bar deadlifts (traditional or sumo stance)
  - Trap bar deadlifts
  - Rack pulls
- Sets: Work up from ~50% 1RM, adding weight each set
- Reps: 5 reps (until you can no longer complete 5 reps)
- Rest: 3-5 minutes

**B. Unilateral Movement**
- Examples: Bulgarian split squats, walking lunges, step-ups
- Sets: 2-3
- Reps: 6-10 per leg
- Rest: 90 seconds

**C. Hamstring/Posterior Chain**
- Examples: Glute-ham raises, Romanian deadlifts, leg curls, back extensions
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 8-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Ground-Based Abdominals**
- Examples: Hanging leg raises, ab wheel rollouts, planks
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 10-15 (or time for planks)
- Rest: 60 seconds

#### Day 3: Repetition Effort Upper Body (or Day 4 in 4-day version)

**A. Repetition Lift** - Work up to 3 sets of max reps
- Examples: Dumbbell bench press, barbell bench press (60-70% 1RM)
- Sets: 3
- Reps: Maximum reps possible
- Rest: 90 seconds between sets

**B/C. Lat/Upper Back Superset**
- B. Vertical pulling: Lat pulldowns, chin-ups, pull-ups
  - Sets: 3-4
  - Reps: 8-12
- C. Upper back: Face pulls, rear delt work, shrugs
  - Sets: 3-4
  - Reps: 8-12
- Rest: Minimal between exercises, 90 seconds between supersets

**D. Biceps**
- Examples: Barbell curls, dumbbell curls, hammer curls
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 8-10
- Rest: 60 seconds

**E. Triceps**
- Examples: Overhead extensions, pushdowns, close grip bench
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 8-10
- Rest: 60 seconds

#### Day 2 (in 4-day version): Dynamic Effort Lower Body

**A. Dynamic Box Squats**
- Sets: 8-10
- Reps: 2
- Weight: 50-60% of 1RM
- Rest: 45-60 seconds
- Technique: Explosive concentric, controlled eccentric
- Optional: Add bands/chains for advanced trainees

**B. Unilateral Movement**
- Same as Max Effort Lower Day

**C. Hamstring/Posterior Chain**
- Same as Max Effort Lower Day

**D. Abdominals**
- Same as Max Effort Lower Day

### Progression

**Max Effort Days:** Rotate the main exercise every 2-3 weeks. The goal is to break your previous record every week on that specific exercise before rotating to a new movement.

**Repetition Days:** When you can complete all prescribed reps easily, add weight (5-10 lbs for upper body, 10-20 lbs for lower body).

**Supplemental/Accessory Work:** Use progressive overload - when you hit the top of the rep range for all sets, increase weight by the smallest increment available.

### Program Duration

- **Minimum commitment:** 12 weeks to see significant results
- **Ongoing:** The program can be run indefinitely by rotating max effort exercises and adjusting accessory work based on weaknesses
- **Deload:** Every 4-6 weeks, take a deload week reducing volume and intensity by 40-50%

### Evidence Base

Joe DeFranco is one of the most respected strength coaches in sports performance, having trained NFL players, combat athletes, and strength sport competitors for over two decades. The program combines:

1. **Max Effort Method:** Scientifically proven to increase maximal strength by recruiting high-threshold motor units
2. **Conjugate Periodization:** Prevents accommodation and keeps the nervous system fresh by rotating exercises
3. **Repetition Method:** Proven effective for hypertrophy through metabolic stress and muscle damage
4. **Frequency:** 3-4 days per week allows for adequate recovery while providing sufficient training stimulus

The program's effectiveness is evidenced by thousands of testimonials from athletes, DeFranco's gym's track record, and its widespread adoption by strength coaches worldwide. It successfully bridges the gap between pure powerlifting programs and bodybuilding splits, making it ideal for athletic development.

## 2. Tactical Barbell

### Name
Tactical Barbell

### Author/Source
**K. Black** (pseudonym for privacy)

**Official Sources:**
- [Tactical Barbell Official Site](https://www.tacticalbarbell.com/)
- Book: "Tactical Barbell: Definitive Strength Training for the Operational Athlete"
- Book: "Tactical Barbell II: Conditioning for the Operational Athlete"

### Description

Tactical Barbell is a minimalist strength and conditioning system specifically designed for military personnel, law enforcement officers, firefighters, and other tactical professionals who need to maintain multiple fitness attributes simultaneously. Unlike sport-specific programs that focus on a single quality (like maximum strength or endurance), Tactical Barbell recognizes that tactical athletes must maintain strength, endurance, work capacity, and tactical skills concurrently.

The program was developed by K. Black, who remains pseudonymous for operational security reasons, based on years of experience in military and tactical operations. The system addresses a critical problem faced by tactical athletes: traditional strength programs often interfere with conditioning and skill work, while endurance programs can decrease strength. Tactical Barbell solves this through minimalist strength templates that build and maintain strength without excessive fatigue, paired with conditioning protocols that enhance work capacity without compromising strength gains.

The philosophy emphasizes sustainability and real-world applicability. Rather than chasing personal records or aesthetic goals, Tactical Barbell focuses on building a robust foundation of strength that can be maintained indefinitely while simultaneously developing the conditioning necessary for operational demands. The program uses sub-maximal training loads and wave periodization to minimize fatigue and injury risk, making it ideal for people who must maintain readiness year-round.

Tactical Barbell consists of two main books: the first covers strength training templates (Fighter, Operator, Zulu, and Mass), while the second covers conditioning protocols (Black, Green, and specialized templates). The beauty of the system is its flexibility - users can mix and match strength and conditioning templates based on their specific needs and operational demands.

### Structure

Tactical Barbell offers multiple templates. The two most popular are:

**Operator Template:** 3 days per week
- Day 1: Squat, Bench Press, Weighted Pull-ups
- Day 2: Squat, Bench Press, Deadlift
- Day 3: Squat, Bench Press, Weighted Pull-ups

**Fighter Template:** 2 days per week
- Day 1: Squat, Bench Press, Overhead Press, Deadlift
- Day 2: Squat, Bench Press, Overhead Press, Deadlift

Both templates run on 6-week cycles using wave periodization.

### Session Breakdown

#### Operator Template (Most Common)

**Training Frequency:** 3x per week (every other day: Mon/Wed/Fri or Tue/Thu/Sat)

**Exercise Selection (Clusters):**
The program uses "clusters" - groups of 3-4 exercises trained together. Most common cluster:
- Squat (back squat or front squat)
- Bench Press (or overhead press)
- Weighted Pull-ups (or deadlift)

Alternative clusters include:
- Front Squat, Overhead Press, Deadlift
- Back Squat, Incline Press, Weighted Pull-ups

**6-Week Wave Progression:**

- **Week 1:** 3 sets x 5 reps @ 70% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

- **Week 2:** 3 sets x 5 reps @ 80% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

- **Week 3:** 3 sets x 3 reps @ 90% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

- **Week 4:** 3 sets x 5 reps @ 75% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

- **Week 5:** 3 sets x 5 reps @ 85% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

- **Week 6:** 3 sets x 2 reps @ 95% 1RM
  - Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets

**Sample Week (Operator):**

*Monday:*
- Back Squat: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Bench Press: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Weighted Pull-ups: 3x5 @ current week's percentage

*Wednesday:*
- Back Squat: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Bench Press: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Deadlift: 3x5 @ current week's percentage (only 2x/week, not all sessions)

*Friday:*
- Back Squat: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Bench Press: 3x5 @ current week's percentage
- Weighted Pull-ups: 3x5 @ current week's percentage

#### Fighter Template

**Training Frequency:** 2x per week (Mon/Fri or Tue/Sat)

**Exercise Selection:**
All four main lifts each session:
- Back Squat
- Bench Press
- Overhead Press
- Deadlift

**Progression:** Same 6-week wave as Operator (70/80/90/75/85/95%)

**Sample Session:**
- Back Squat: 3x3 @ 90% 1RM (Week 3 example)
- Bench Press: 3x3 @ 90% 1RM
- Overhead Press: 3x3 @ 90% 1RM
- Deadlift: 3x3 @ 90% 1RM
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between exercises

### Progression

**Linear Progression within 6-week blocks:**
Intensity increases each week following the wave structure, then resets with higher maxes for the next block.

**Testing Maxes:**
- Retest 1RM or 3RM every 6-12 weeks
- Use new maxes to calculate training percentages for next block

**Training Max Option:**
If percentages feel too heavy, calculate using 90% of true 1RM as your "training max"

**Long-term Progression:**
The program is designed to be run continuously. After each 6-week block, test new maxes and begin another cycle. Deload weeks are built into the wave structure (Week 4 is effectively a deload).

### Program Duration

- **Cycle Length:** 6 weeks per block
- **Testing:** Every 6-12 weeks
- **Long-term:** Designed to run indefinitely
- **Deload:** Built into the wave (Week 4)
- **Minimum commitment:** 2-3 blocks (12-18 weeks) to assess effectiveness

### Evidence Base

Tactical Barbell is highly respected in military, law enforcement, and first responder communities for several reasons:

1. **Real-world Validation:** Widely used by active duty military, law enforcement, and tactical professionals who must maintain readiness while managing operational stress

2. **Sub-maximal Training:** Research supports that training at 70-90% loads with proper periodization builds strength effectively while minimizing fatigue and injury risk

3. **Wave Periodization:** The 3-week wave structure is supported by periodization research showing that varied intensity prevents accommodation and overtraining

4. **Minimalism:** Limited exercise selection (3-4 movements) allows for technical mastery and reduces recovery demands, enabling concurrent conditioning work

5. **Frequency and Volume:** 2-3 days per week with moderate volume is optimal for strength maintenance and modest gains without interfering with other training demands

6. **Sustainability:** Designed for year-round training rather than peaking for competition, which aligns with tactical athlete needs

The program's effectiveness is demonstrated by its widespread adoption in tactical communities, countless user testimonials, and alignment with strength training research. It exemplifies Dan John's concept of "easy strength" - getting stronger without destroying yourself.

### Exercise Library Notes

**Exercises likely in database:**
- Squat, Deadlift, Bench press, Overhead press

**Exercises possibly missing:**
- Front squat (variant of squat)
- Weighted pull-ups (pull-up with added weight)
- Weighted dips (dip with added weight)

**Special Notes:**
- Program emphasizes barbell movements exclusively
- No accessory work or isolation exercises
- All exercises should be compound movements with full range of motion

## 3. Starting Strength (with Power Clean)

### Name
Starting Strength Novice Program

### Author/Source
**Mark Rippetoe** - Strength coach, author, and owner of Starting Strength Gyms

**Official Sources:**
- [Starting Strength Official Site](https://startingstrength.com/)
- Book: "Starting Strength: Basic Barbell Training" (3rd Edition)
- [Starting Strength Programs](https://startingstrength.com/get-started/programs)

### Description

Starting Strength is arguably the most famous novice barbell training program in the world, designed by Mark Rippetoe, a legendary strength coach with over 40 years of experience. The program is built on a simple but profound principle: untrained individuals (novices) can recover from and adapt to training stress within 48-72 hours, allowing them to add weight to the bar every single workout for an extended period - a phenomenon called "linear progression" or the "novice effect."

The program uses only five barbell exercises: the squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, and power clean, with chin-ups as the primary assistance exercise. This minimalist approach isn't arbitrary - these movements were selected because they train the entire body through functional movement patterns, allow for progressive loading across a long range of motion, and produce the greatest strength adaptations per unit of time invested.

Regarding Olympic lifts, Rippetoe has a specific philosophy: the power clean is valuable for teaching explosive hip extension and training the nervous system to produce power, making it superior to accessory exercises for athletic development. However, the competitive Olympic lifts (snatch and clean & jerk) are NOT recommended for the general population. Rippetoe argues that these full lifts require extensive technical coaching, don't produce strength gains superior to squats and deadlifts, and aren't worth the time investment for non-competitive weightlifters.

The power clean serves a specific purpose in Starting Strength: it teaches rapid force production and provides a training stimulus that enhances deadlift performance while developing explosive power. It's programmed on alternate days from deadlifts, typically after the trainee has built a foundation of strength in the basic lifts. The program's brilliance lies in its simplicity - by focusing on progressive overload on a handful of movements, novices can achieve remarkable strength gains in 3-9 months before requiring more sophisticated programming.

### Structure

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4):**
Two alternating workouts (A/B), 3x per week

**Workout A:**
- Squat
- Press (Overhead Press)
- Deadlift

**Workout B:**
- Squat
- Bench Press
- Deadlift

**Phase 2 (After Week 4-8, when ready for power cleans):**

**Workout A:**
- Squat
- Press (Overhead Press)
- Deadlift (once per week, alternates with power clean)
- Chin-ups: 3 sets to failure or weighted

**Workout B:**
- Squat
- Bench Press
- Power Clean (alternates with deadlift)
- Chin-ups: 3 sets to failure or weighted

**Weekly Schedule Example (Phase 2):**
- Monday: Squat, Press, Deadlift, Chin-ups
- Wednesday: Squat, Bench, Power Clean, Chin-ups
- Friday: Squat, Press, Power Clean, Chin-ups
- Monday: Squat, Bench, Deadlift, Chin-ups
- Wednesday: Squat, Press, Power Clean, Chin-ups
- (pattern continues)

### Session Breakdown

#### Core Exercises - Standard Protocol

**Squat** (Every session)
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 5
- Weight: Add 5-10 lbs per session (depends on bodyweight and training age)
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets
- Notes: Low-bar back squat to proper depth (hip crease below top of knee)

**Deadlift** (Once per week, alternates with power clean)
- Sets: 1
- Reps: 5
- Weight: Add 10-20 lbs per session initially, then 5-10 lbs
- Rest: 5+ minutes after set (it's the last exercise)
- Notes: Conventional or sumo stance based on anatomy

**Bench Press** (1-2x per week)
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 5
- Weight: Add 5-10 lbs per session initially, then 2.5-5 lbs
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets
- Notes: Competition-style with proper arch and leg drive

**Overhead Press** (1-2x per week)
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 5
- Weight: Add 5 lbs per session initially, then 2.5 lbs, eventually microloading
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets
- Notes: Strict press, no push press

**Power Clean** (1-2x per week, alternates with deadlift)
- Sets: 5
- Reps: 3
- Weight: Add 5-10 lbs per session
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets
- Notes: Focus on technique; this is a technical, explosive movement

**Chin-ups/Pull-ups** (Assistance exercise)
- Sets: 3
- Reps: To failure initially, then weighted sets of 5-10
- Weight: Add weight when bodyweight sets exceed 10-15 reps
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between sets
- Notes: Can substitute with lat pulldowns initially if unable to do chin-ups

#### Sample Week (Phase 2 - Intermediate Novice)

**Monday (A):**
1. Squat: 3x5 @ 225 lbs (add 5 lbs from previous workout)
2. Overhead Press: 3x5 @ 105 lbs (add 2.5 lbs from previous workout)
3. Deadlift: 1x5 @ 275 lbs (add 10 lbs from previous deadlift session)
4. Chin-ups: 3x8-10

**Wednesday (B):**
1. Squat: 3x5 @ 230 lbs
2. Bench Press: 3x5 @ 165 lbs
3. Power Clean: 5x3 @ 135 lbs
4. Chin-ups: 3x8-10

**Friday (A):**
1. Squat: 3x5 @ 235 lbs
2. Overhead Press: 3x5 @ 107.5 lbs
3. Power Clean: 5x3 @ 140 lbs
4. Chin-ups: 3x8-10

### Progression

**Linear Progression (The Novice Effect):**
Add weight every single workout according to these guidelines:

**Squat:**
- First 2-3 workouts: +10 lbs
- Next several weeks: +5 lbs per session
- When progress slows: Switch to 2.5 lbs per session or implement Texas Method/intermediate programming

**Deadlift:**
- First 2-3 deadlift sessions: +15-20 lbs
- Next several sessions: +10 lbs
- When progress slows: +5 lbs per session
- Eventually: Deadlift every other week with +10 lbs jumps

**Bench Press:**
- First workout: +10 lbs (maybe)
- Most workouts: +5 lbs per session
- When progress slows: +2.5 lbs per session, then microloading (1-2 lbs)

**Overhead Press:**
- Most sessions: +5 lbs initially
- Quickly moves to: +2.5 lbs per session
- Eventually: Microloading with fractional plates (0.5-1 lb increments)

**Power Clean:**
- Start light (95-135 lbs for most males)
- Add 5-10 lbs per session
- Technique should remain crisp; if form degrades, stay at same weight

**When to Reset:**
When you fail to complete 3x5 for three consecutive sessions on a lift, deload 10-20% and work back up with smaller jumps.

**Novice Program Duration:**
Most trainees can run linear progression for 3-9 months depending on:
- Age (younger trainees progress longer)
- Body weight (heavier people progress longer)
- Recovery (diet, sleep, stress)
- Starting strength level

### Program Duration

- **Novice Phase:** 3-9 months of linear progression
- **Frequency:** 3 days per week
- **Session duration:** 45-90 minutes
- **After novice phase:** Transition to Texas Method, Heavy Light Medium, or other intermediate programming

### Evidence Base

Starting Strength is one of the most evidence-based and time-tested novice programs for several reasons:

1. **Proven Track Record:** Tens of thousands of novices have documented their progression from the program, with typical male trainees reaching 300+ lb squats, 400+ lb deadlifts, and 200+ lb bench presses within 6-12 months

2. **Biomechanical Efficiency:** The exercise selection maximizes force production through the longest effective range of motion, creating optimal strength adaptation stimulus

3. **Linear Progression Science:** Research supports that untrained individuals can adapt to progressive overload on very short timescales (48-72 hours), validating the daily progression model

4. **Compound Movements:** Scientific literature consistently shows compound, multi-joint barbell movements produce superior strength and muscle gains compared to isolation exercises for novices

5. **Power Clean Rationale:** Research demonstrates that explosive lifting movements enhance rate of force development (power) and improve performance in athletic movements requiring quick force production. The power clean is simpler to learn than full Olympic lifts while providing similar power development benefits

6. **Minimal Effective Dose:** The program exemplifies training efficiency - maximum results from minimum time investment, which improves adherence

7. **Systematic Coaching:** Rippetoe's coaching methodology, documented in the Starting Strength book and seminars, provides objective standards for movement quality

**Limitations and When to Use Olympic Lifts:**
Rippetoe explicitly states that competitive Olympic lifting (snatch, clean & jerk) is NOT beneficial for general strength development and should only be pursued by those interested in the sport itself. The power clean is the only Olympic lift variation included because it provides unique benefits (explosive hip extension, power development) while being learnable in a few sessions with proper coaching.

**Exercises likely in database:**

**Exercises possibly missing:**
- Power clean (Olympic lift variation)
- Chin-up (bodyweight pulling)
- Pull-up (wider grip variant of chin-up)

**Special Notes:**
- Low-bar back squat is the specific squat variation used (not high-bar)
- Strict overhead press (not push press or jerk)
- Conventional or sumo deadlift based on individual anatomy
- Power clean is from the floor, not from hang position

## 4. Simple and Sinister

### Name
Simple and Sinister (often abbreviated as S&S)

### Author/Source
**Pavel Tsatsouline** - Former Soviet Special Forces instructor, Chairman of StrongFirst

**Official Sources:**
- Book: "Kettlebell Simple & Sinister: Revised and Updated (2nd Edition)"
- [StrongFirst Official Site](https://www.strongfirst.com/)
- [Sinister Achievement Standards](https://www.strongfirst.com/achieve/sinister/)

### Description

Simple and Sinister is a minimalist kettlebell program designed by Pavel Tsatsouline, the man credited with bringing Russian kettlebell training to the West. The program embodies Pavel's philosophy of "strength as a skill" and demonstrates that extraordinary results can be achieved through perfect execution of just two exercises: the kettlebell swing and the Turkish get-up.

The program's genius lies in its extreme simplicity and focus on movement quality over quantity. Every session consists of exactly 100 one-arm kettlebell swings (10 sets of 10) and 10 Turkish get-ups (5 per side). That's it. No periodization, no complex programming, no variety. You perform this same workout 5-6 days per week, progressively using heavier kettlebells as you meet specific time and weight standards.

Pavel designed Simple and Sinister to develop what he calls "strong endurance" - the ability to generate significant force repeatedly over time without fatigue. The kettlebell swing builds explosive hip power and posterior chain strength through ballistic training, while the Turkish get-up develops full-body stability, mobility, and strength through a complex, controlled movement pattern. Together, these movements create remarkable carryover to real-world activities, athletic performance, and injury resilience.

The revised edition (S&S 2.0) clarified the progression structure, introducing the "Timeless Simple" standard as the first milestone before attempting the timed "Simple" and "Sinister" standards. This addresses a common mistake where practitioners rush progression. Pavel emphasizes that this program is not a quick fix but a practice - something you can do for years, constantly refining technique while building an indestructible foundation of strength and conditioning.

What makes Simple and Sinister unique in the landscape of strength programs is its sustainability and accessibility. The entire workout takes 20-30 minutes, requires minimal equipment (just a few kettlebells), and produces minimal fatigue, making it ideal for people who need to maintain strength and conditioning alongside demanding work, sport training, or martial arts practice.

### Structure

**Frequency:** 5-6 days per week

**Session Structure:**
Every session is identical:

1. **Warm-up:** Prying goblet squat, halos, hip bridges (5-10 minutes)
2. **Swings:** 10 sets of 10 reps (alternating arms every 10 reps)
3. **Rest/Recovery:** 1 minute
4. **Turkish Get-ups:** 10 total reps (1 rep per side, alternating, 5 each side)
5. **Cool-down:** Light stretching (optional)

**No rest days required:** The program is designed to be sustainable daily. Take rest days as needed based on life demands, not because the program requires it.

### Session Breakdown

#### Warm-up (5-10 minutes)

**Prying Goblet Squat:**
- Reps: 5-10
- Notes: Hold bottom position, "pry" knees out with elbows, focus on hip mobility

**Halos:**
- Reps: 5 each direction
- Notes: Circle kettlebell around head, keep abs tight, improve shoulder mobility

**Hip Bridges:**
- Reps: 10-20
- Notes: Glute activation, spine extension

#### Main Work

**One-Arm Kettlebell Swings**
- Sets: 10
- Reps: 10 per set (alternating arms: 10L, 10R, 10L, 10R, etc.)
- Total: 100 swings
- Rest: Every 30 seconds perform a set of 10 (on the minute)
- Technique: Hardstyle swing, explosive hip snap, horizontal trajectory
- Special notes:
  - Use a timer
  - 10 swings on the 0:00, 0:30, 1:00, 1:30, etc.
  - Complete all 10 sets in 5 minutes for "Simple" standard
  - Breathing: Forceful exhale at top (like blowing out candles)

**Rest Break:** 1 minute after completing all swings

**Turkish Get-up (TGU)**
- Sets: 10 (1 rep per set)
- Reps: 1 rep per set, alternating sides
- Total: 10 get-ups (5 left, 5 right)
- Pattern: 1L, 1R, 1L, 1R, 1L, 1R, 1L, 1R, 1L, 1R
- Rest: Perform 1 get-up every minute (on the minute for 10 minutes)
- Technique: Slow, controlled, maintain vertical arm with kettlebell
- Special notes:
  - Each rep should be deliberate and perfect
  - Complete all 10 reps in 10 minutes for "Simple" standard
  - Practice both sides equally
  - This is a movement practice, not a grind

#### Total Workout Time
- Timeless Simple: No time limit, focus on quality
- Simple Standard: ~15 minutes (5 min swings + 1 min rest + 10 min get-ups)
- Sinister Standard: ~15 minutes with heavier weights
- Including warm-up: 20-30 minutes total

Simple and Sinister uses weight-based progression with specific standards:

#### How to Progress

**"Leap of Faith" Progression:**
Pavel recommends large jumps in weight (8 kg/18 lbs) rather than small increments. When you achieve a standard with one weight, jump to the next heavier bell.

**Timeline:**

- Simple Standard may take 1-2 years
- Sinister Standard may take 3-5+ years or may never be achieved (and that's okay)

**Testing:**

- **Deload:** Not required; take rest days as needed based on life demands

## 5. Conjugate Method for General Population

### Name
The Conjugate Method (Adapted for General Population)

### Author/Source
**Louie Simmons** - Founder of Westside Barbell (original developer)
**Various Coaches** - Adapted for non-powerlifters (Jim Wendler, Joe DeFranco, CJ Murphy, and others)

**Official Sources:**
- [Westside Barbell Conjugate Method](https://www.westside-barbell.com/pages/conjugate-method)
- [Understanding the Conjugate System](https://www.westside-barbell.com/blogs/the-blog/conjugate-system)
- [Conjugate Method Walkthrough](https://www.westside-barbell.com/blogs/the-blog/starting-conjugate-training-walkthrough)

### Description

The Conjugate Method is a revolutionary training system developed by Louie Simmons at Westside Barbell, based on Soviet and Bulgarian periodization research. While originally designed for elite powerlifters, the principles have been adapted for general population strength training, athletic development, and tactical fitness. The method's core innovation is the simultaneous development of multiple strength qualities through varied training stresses - what Simmons calls "concurrent periodization."

Traditional periodization requires athletes to focus on one quality at a time (hypertrophy, then strength, then power), which can lead to detraining of previously developed qualities. The Conjugate Method solves this by training maximum strength, dynamic strength (speed-strength), and repetition work for hypertrophy within the same week, preventing accommodation (the body's adaptation to repeated stimuli) and maintaining all qualities simultaneously.

The system revolves around three training methods borrowed from Soviet research: the Maximum Effort Method (working up to maximal loads to develop absolute strength), the Dynamic Effort Method (moving sub-maximal loads with maximal speed to develop rate of force development), and the Repetition Method (performing exercises to muscular fatigue for hypertrophy and work capacity). These methods are applied through a 4-day upper/lower split with strategic exercise rotation.

For the general population, the Conjugate Method must be simplified from Westside's advanced protocols. Elite powerlifters at Westside use specialized equipment (chains, bands, specialty bars), max out on exercises weekly, and train with volumes that would destroy most people. Adapted versions reduce frequency of true max efforts, simplify exercise selection, reduce overall volume, and remove the need for specialty equipment while maintaining the core principles of varied training stress and concurrent development.

The beauty of the Conjugate Method for athletes and general population is its flexibility and address of weaknesses. By rotating exercises every 1-3 weeks, you prevent accommodation and overuse injuries. The variety also keeps training engaging, which improves long-term adherence. Most importantly, it develops well-rounded strength and athleticism rather than just getting better at a few specific lifts.

### Structure

**4-Day Upper/Lower Split:**

- **Day 1:** Max Effort Lower Body (Monday)
- **Day 2:** Max Effort Upper Body (Wednesday)
- **Day 3:** Dynamic Effort Lower Body (Friday)
- **Day 4:** Dynamic Effort Upper Body (Saturday or Sunday)

**Recovery Pattern:**
- Max effort days are separated to allow CNS recovery
- Dynamic days are less taxing and can be done on consecutive days if needed
- At least 48-72 hours between training the same muscle groups

### Session Breakdown

#### Day 1: Max Effort Lower Body

**A. Max Effort Exercise** - Work up to 1-3 rep max
- Exercise rotation (changes every 1-3 weeks):
  - Box squat (various heights)
  - Free squat (various stances)
  - Front squat
  - Safety squat bar squat
  - Deadlift (conventional)
  - Deadlift (sumo)
  - Rack pulls
  - Good mornings
- Protocol: Work up from 50-60% in sets of 3-5 reps, eventually reaching 1-3RM
- Sets: 5-8 working up to max
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between heavy sets

**B. Supplemental Lower Body Exercise**
- Examples: Lunges, split squats, step-ups, belt squats
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 6-10
- Rest: 2 minutes

**C. Posterior Chain/Hamstring**
- Examples: Romanian deadlifts, glute-ham raises, back extensions, leg curls
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 8-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Abs/Core**
- Examples: Weighted sit-ups, ab wheel, hanging leg raises
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 10-20
- Rest: 60 seconds

**Total Accessory Exercises:** 3-6 movements after max effort lift

#### Day 2: Max Effort Upper Body

**A. Max Effort Exercise** - Work up to 1-3 rep max
- Exercise rotation (changes every 1-3 weeks):
  - Bench press (various grips)
  - Floor press
  - Incline bench press (various angles)
  - Board press (2-board, 3-board, 4-board)
  - Close-grip bench press
  - Overhead press
  - Incline press
- Protocol: Work up from 50-60% in sets of 3-5 reps, eventually reaching 1-3RM
- Sets: 5-8 working up to max
- Rest: 3-5 minutes between heavy sets

**B. Supplemental Pressing**
- Examples: Dumbbell bench, dumbbell incline, overhead press variations
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 6-10
- Rest: 2 minutes

**C. Horizontal Pulling**
- Examples: Barbell rows, dumbbell rows, chest-supported rows, seated cable rows
- Sets: 4-5
- Reps: 8-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Vertical Pulling**
- Examples: Chin-ups, pull-ups, lat pulldowns
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 6-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**E. Rear Delts/Upper Back**
- Examples: Face pulls, rear delt flyes, band pull-aparts
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 12-15
- Rest: 60 seconds

**F. Arms (optional)**
- Triceps: Pushdowns, overhead extensions, close-grip pressing
- Biceps: Curls, hammer curls
- Sets: 2-3 each
- Reps: 10-15
- Rest: 60 seconds

**Total Accessory Exercises:** 4-6 movements after max effort lift

#### Day 3: Dynamic Effort Lower Body

**A. Dynamic Effort Squats**
- Exercise: Box squats or free squats
- Sets: 8-12
- Reps: 2
- Weight: 50-60% of 1RM (without bands/chains)
- Rest: 45-60 seconds
- Tempo: Explosive concentric (stand up as fast as possible), controlled eccentric
- Notes: Focus on speed, not grinding reps

**B. Dynamic Effort Deadlifts** (optional, can substitute speed pulls)
- Sets: 6-8
- Reps: 1
- Weight: 60-70% of 1RM
- Rest: 60 seconds
- Tempo: Explosive from floor
- Notes: Can use deficit deadlifts, rack pulls, or regular deadlifts

**C. Unilateral/Accessory Lower**
- Examples: Bulgarian split squats, walking lunges, single-leg press
- Sets: 3
- Reps: 8-10 per leg
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Posterior Chain**
- Examples: Glute-ham raises, back extensions, leg curls, pull-throughs
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 10-15
- Rest: 60-90 seconds

**E. Abs/Core**
- Examples: Planks, ab wheel, cable crunches
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 10-20 or time
- Rest: 60 seconds

**Total Accessory Exercises:** 2-4 movements after dynamic work

#### Day 4: Dynamic Effort Upper Body

**A. Dynamic Effort Bench Press**
- Exercise: Bench press with 3 different grips
- Sets: 9 total (3 sets each of 3 grips)
- Reps: 3
- Weight: 50-60% of 1RM
- Rest: 45-60 seconds between sets
- Tempo: Explosive concentric, controlled eccentric
- Grip variations:
  - Close grip (pinky on smooth)
  - Medium grip (index on rings)
  - Wide grip (outside rings)
- Notes: Move the bar as fast as possible; if bar speed slows, reduce weight

**B. Vertical Pulling**
- Examples: Weighted chin-ups, pull-ups, lat pulldowns
- Sets: 4-5
- Reps: 6-10
- Rest: 90 seconds

**C. Horizontal Pulling**
- Examples: Barbell rows, cable rows, dumbbell rows
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 8-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**D. Shoulders**
- Examples: Dumbbell press, lateral raises, overhead press
- Sets: 3-4
- Reps: 8-12
- Rest: 90 seconds

**E. Arms**
- Triceps: Overhead extensions, pushdowns, dips
- Biceps: Curls, hammer curls
- Sets: 3-4 each
- Reps: 10-15
- Rest: 60 seconds

**Total Accessory Exercises:** 2-4 movements after dynamic work

### Progression

#### Max Effort Progression

**Exercise Rotation:**
- Rotate max effort exercise every 1-3 weeks (Westside rotates weekly, general population can keep for 2-3 weeks)
- Goal: Beat your previous record on that specific exercise variant
- When returning to an exercise after 6-8 weeks, aim to exceed your previous max

**Wave Loading Example (for general population):**
- Week 1: Work up to 3RM on Box Squat
- Week 2: Work up to 2RM on Box Squat
- Week 3: Work up to 1RM on Box Squat
- Week 4: Rotate to new exercise (e.g., Front Squat 3RM)

#### Dynamic Effort Progression

**Speed Work:**
- Weight stays constant (50-60% of 1RM) for 3 weeks
- Week 4: Retest max and recalculate percentages OR rotate exercise
- Focus on bar speed, not absolute weight
- Advanced: Add bands or chains (5-20% additional load at top)

**General Population Option:**
Some coaches recommend replacing dynamic effort bench with repetition work for non-powerlifters, as most people need more muscle mass, not more speed work.

#### Accessory Work Progression

- When you hit top of rep range for all sets, add weight
- Use double progression (increase reps, then weight)
- Track all lifts to ensure progressive overload

### Program Duration

- **Minimum commitment:** 12-16 weeks to adapt to the system
- **Typical blocks:** Run continuously with deloads every 4-6 weeks
- **Deload protocol:** Reduce volume by 40-50%, skip max effort (work up to 80-85% only)
- **Long-term:** Can be run indefinitely with proper exercise rotation and deloads
- **Testing:** Don't test true 1RMs on competition lifts often - the max effort variants build the competition lifts

### Evidence Base

The Conjugate Method is highly respected in strength and conditioning for several reasons:

1. **Louie Simmons' Legacy:** Westside Barbell has produced more elite powerlifters (800+ lb squatters, 600+ lb benchers, 900+ lb deadlifters) than any other gym, validating the method's effectiveness for maximum strength

2. **Scientific Foundation:** Based on Soviet sports science research, particularly the work of A.S. Prilepin, Y.V. Verkhoshansky, and Bulgarian weightlifting methodology

3. **Concurrent Periodization:** Research supports training multiple qualities simultaneously to prevent detraining and maintain year-round readiness

4. **Maximum Effort Method:** Working up to maximal loads (1-3RM) is proven to:
   - Recruit high-threshold motor units
   - Improve neural efficiency
   - Build absolute strength
   - Increase bone density

5. **Dynamic Effort Method:** Speed work at 50-60% loads develops:
   - Rate of force development (RFD)
   - Starting strength
   - Explosive power
   - Technical proficiency under fatigue

6. **Exercise Rotation:** Prevents:
   - Accommodation (diminishing returns from repeated stimulus)
   - Overuse injuries
   - Mental staleness
   - Technical plateaus

7. **Repetition Method:** High-rep work to fatigue produces:
   - Hypertrophy through metabolic stress
   - Work capacity
   - Technique practice with fatigue
   - Structural balance

8. **Athletic Applications:** While developed for powerlifting, the principles have been successfully adapted for:
   - Football (NFL strength coaches use conjugate principles)
   - MMA and combat sports
   - Track and field
   - Military/tactical fitness
   - General strength and conditioning

**Adaptations for General Population:**

The raw Westside method is NOT appropriate for most people. Adaptations include:
- Reducing max effort frequency (every 2-3 weeks instead of weekly)
- Simplifying exercise selection (no specialty bars required)
- Reducing total volume (fewer accessory exercises)
- Removing or minimizing accommodating resistance (bands/chains)
- Longer exercise rotations (2-3 weeks instead of weekly)
- Modified dynamic effort protocols (some coaches replace with repetition work for hypertrophy)

**For General Population:**
Programs like Westside for Skinny Bastards (covered above) represent better implementations of conjugate principles for non-elite lifters.

## Conclusion

These five programs represent different philosophies and methodologies in evidence-based strength training, but all share common principles: progressive overload, compound movements, and systematic programming. The choice between them depends on individual goals, experience level, available time, equipment access, and concurrent training demands.

For a comprehensive workout app, implementing these programs would provide users with proven, time-tested options covering the spectrum from beginner (Starting Strength) to advanced (Conjugate Method), from barbell-focused (Tactical Barbell) to kettlebell-based (Simple & Sinister), and from pure strength (Starting Strength) to strength + size (WS4SB).

Each program has produced countless success stories and is backed by coaches with decades of experience and thousands of trained athletes. They represent the gold standard in their respective niches within strength and conditioning.
